Why this matters

Cultural Heritage, Carried Forward

01 — Living Memory

Indigenous art is not decoration — it is memory made visible. Each motif encodes a community's relationship with land, ritual, and lineage, refined across centuries of hands.

02 — Quiet Disappearance

As fewer households practice these traditions daily, the risk isn't dramatic loss — it's quiet erosion. Documentation and access are now part of how these art forms survive.

03 — Digital Preservation

This project archives history, technique, and meaning alongside the artwork itself — so the "why" travels with the "what," for students, researchers, and the curious alike.

The Collection

Three Traditions

Maharashtra

Warli Art

Painted in white rice-paste on mud-red walls, Warli reduces the world to circle, triangle, and square — and from that spare vocabulary builds entire harvests, weddings, and dances.

Origin
Warli tribal communities, Sahyadri hills
Materials
Rice paste, gum, bamboo stick brush, mud wall or paper
GI Status
Registered — Warli Paintings of Nashik District

Bihar

Madhubani Art

Born in the Mithila region, Madhubani fills every inch of its surface — fish, peacocks, lotus, deity — in bold outline and unbroken color, a style that refuses empty space.

Origin
Mithila region, Darbhanga & Madhubani districts
Materials
Natural pigments — turmeric, indigo, sindoor, soot
GI Status
Registered — Madhubani Paintings, 2007

Karnataka

Kasuti Embroidery

Worked thread by counted thread with no knot ever tied, Kasuti renders temple towers and palanquins onto cloth — a needlework discipline once essential to a bride's trousseau.

Origin
Northern Karnataka — Dharwad, Hubli, Gadag
Materials
Silk/cotton thread on dark cotton or silk fabric
GI Status
Registered — Kasuti Embroidery, 2005

Behind the Work

Artist & Community Stories

Maharashtra · Warli

Jivya Soma Mashe

Widely credited with bringing Warli painting from village walls to the global art world in the 1970s, while keeping its ritual grammar intact.

Bihar · Madhubani

The Women of Mithila

Madhubani has always been a matrilineal art — taught mother to daughter as household knowledge, long before it reached paper or galleries.

Karnataka · Kasuti

The Lingayat Embroiderers

Once essential to a bride's trousseau, Kasuti nearly disappeared in the 20th century before state-backed revival programs brought it back.
